# Overnight FX Information Transfer at the Tokyo Open

A research project from Mathematics 86 (Mathematical Finance) at Dartmouth College, completed in Winter 2026 with Ken Hayashi and Taiki Takahashi. The full paper is available in the repository; what follows is a short description of what we built, what we found, and what we think it teaches.

The question started from a simple observation about market microstructure. Foreign exchange trades continuously, around the clock, across overlapping regional sessions. Equity markets trade only during their local hours. When these timings differ, a structural gap opens between the two venues — information priced into FX during the window when equities are closed cannot be reflected in equity prices until the equity market reopens. For firms whose underlying economics depend on FX — say, a Japanese mining company earning USD-denominated metals revenue against JPY-denominated operating costs — that gap should produce a predictable repricing event at the opening auction.

The paper tests this idea using three Japanese non-ferrous miners (Sumitomo Metal Mining, Mitsubishi Materials, and Mitsui Mining & Smelting) and three years of daily equity data paired against 5-minute USD/JPY bars. The identification strategy is a strict timestamp-level lookahead guard: no FX bar dated at or after the 09:00 Tokyo opening auction enters the feature set. A hard runtime assertion enforces this per-row, not just at the aggregate level. The point of that discipline is to ensure that any predictive relationship we find cannot be an artifact of bidirectional intraday price discovery between FX and equities. If we see information flowing from overnight FX into the opening gap, it is flowing in one direction only.

We ran seven experimental configurations — three single-stock models and four multi-stock baskets — and a two-layer econometric framework on each. The parametric layer was HAC OLS with Newey-West standard errors, fit in both a Core specification (FX volatility and return) and a Full specification (adding 2-day and 5-day cumulative returns). The non-parametric layer was a Random Forest regressor for gap magnitude and a classifier for direction, both with 500 trees and a fixed random seed. Beyond the point estimates, we computed cross-correlation functions at lags 0 through 5, Granger causality F-tests at lags 1 through 5, VAR(2) orthogonalized impulse response functions with bootstrap confidence bands, and expanding-window rolling out-of-sample R² series to check temporal stability.

The headline finding is straightforward: overnight FX returns carry statistically significant information about the next-morning opening gap. The standardized HAC coefficient on 1-day FX return is between 0.164 and 0.227 across all seven configurations, with Newey-West p-values between 0.0009 and 0.0248. The effect is robust — it holds whether the sample is one firm, two firms, or all three; whether we use the Core specification or the Full specification; whether we control for autoregressive gap structure or not. FX volatility is statistically insignificant in every linear specification, but ranks as the second-most-important feature in the Random Forest models, which we interpret as volatility operating as a conditioning variable on the transmission of directional FX signals rather than as a direct driver of gap magnitude.

The more interesting finding is what the effect doesn't do. Granger causality fails at every lag beyond the contemporaneous day, with p-values clustering between 0.20 and 0.52. The impulse response functions show a sign reversal at step one and decay to zero by step four. Out-of-sample R² for magnitude prediction is negative across all seven experiments, ranging from -0.065 to -0.178 — meaning a naive zero forecast outperforms the Random Forest regressor in every case. Directional accuracy is only meaningfully above chance in one of seven configurations (60.1% on Mitsubishi Materials); the other six cluster near a coin flip. Taken together, this pattern is exactly what a bounded information-friction effect should look like: FX information accumulates while Tokyo is closed, is absorbed at the opening batch auction, and leaves no residual predictive power for subsequent sessions.

We deliberately stopped short of proposing a trading strategy. The paper's own empirical pattern — significant contemporaneous effect, null Granger causality at all longer lags, negative OOS R², sign-reversing impulse response — establishes that the effect is too local to the opening auction for naive daily-frequency strategies to capture. Section 7 of the paper sketches how a quanto derivative framework could isolate the equity-specific repricing signal from spot FX exposure, which we think is the natural next step if the finding were to be translated into an executable strategy. But that extension is presented as future work rather than as a validated backtest, and we are careful throughout the paper to keep the substantive and methodological claims separate.

The contribution, as we understand it, is methodological more than economic. The paper shows that a carefully-identified lead-lag hypothesis can be rigorously characterized — with a strict lookahead guard, a seven-configuration cross-validation, a reconciliation of linear and non-linear evidence, and a full suite of time-series diagnostics — without overclaiming. The boundedness of the effect is the finding. The negative OOS R² is not a problem; it is evidence that the analysis is behaving as a properly-identified information-friction study should behave. For a reader evaluating this work on research-contribution terms, the methodological discipline is the point. A version of this paper that claimed tradability would be less credible, not more, because the underlying effect is too local to the opening auction to support that claim.

What I learned, separately from the paper's substantive findings, is that the discipline of writing down what you can and cannot conclude is worth as much as the analysis itself. The instinct, working on research as a student, is to phrase findings as strongly as the data will tolerate. The opposite instinct — to phrase findings as narrowly as the data actually justify, to leave space in the paper for what the evidence doesn't support — produces cleaner, more useful work. Every time I caught myself wanting to soften the "out-of-sample magnitude prediction is worse than a zero forecast" language, the paper got a little worse as a result. Keeping that language blunt is what lets the substantive contemporaneous finding carry its full weight.
