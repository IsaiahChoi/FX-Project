# Methodology

A technical but accessible companion to the paper, written for readers who want to understand the research design without working through the formal derivations.

## The question

Financial information does not propagate through the world uniformly. Foreign exchange markets trade continuously across overlapping regional sessions; equity markets trade only during their local hours. When these timings differ, a structural gap opens: information priced into FX during the window when equities are closed cannot be reflected in equity prices until the equity market reopens. The paper asks whether that information, observed strictly before the Tokyo Stock Exchange's 09:00 JST opening auction, has any predictive content for the opening gap of USD-exposed Japanese equities.

The three firms studied — Sumitomo Metal Mining, Mitsubishi Materials, and Mitsui Mining & Smelting — were selected because they share a common economic channel to the USD/JPY exchange rate. Each derives substantial revenue from metal sales priced globally in USD, while maintaining operating costs and liabilities denominated in JPY. When USD strengthens overnight, the JPY-denominated value of these firms' dollar cash flows mechanically rises. If the Tokyo market were open, that repricing would happen continuously; because it is closed, the repricing must happen discretely, at the opening auction.

## What we measure

The dependent variable is the opening gap, defined as the percentage change from the previous trading day's close to the current day's opening price. The predictors are derived entirely from FX data recorded during the overnight window — specifically, bars between 15:00 JST on the prior trading day and 08:50 JST on the prediction date. We compute the overnight FX return as the sum of 5-minute log returns over this window, the realized volatility as the standard deviation of those returns, and two additional features capturing multi-day persistence: the 2-day and 5-day cumulative returns. We also include lagged opening gaps (t-1, t-2) to control for autoregressive structure in the dependent variable and day-of-week dummies to absorb weekly seasonality.

A rigorous lookahead guard serves as the identification device. At the dataframe level, no FX bar with a timestamp at or after 09:00 JST on the prediction date enters the feature set. At runtime, the pipeline asserts this condition per row and raises a hard error if violated. This strict timestamp cutoff is what distinguishes the analysis from a conventional same-day correlation study: the predictive content is purely unidirectional, from FX to equity, because FX information after the equity open cannot have influenced the training of the model.

Gaps with absolute value below 10^-5 are truncated to zero to mitigate mechanical rounding artifacts introduced by Bloomberg's pricing conventions. This is a conservative data-cleaning step that affects a small minority of observations and is documented in the code review notes for reviewers who want to evaluate its impact on the results.

## What we tested

The paper reports seven experimental configurations: three single-stock models, three two-stock baskets, and one three-stock basket. In the multi-stock configurations, equity-specific dummy variables are included to absorb firm-level effects that would otherwise confound the cross-sectional pooled coefficient on FX return. The seven-way design is deliberately more than a typical single-model paper; the motivation is to test whether any apparent lead-lag pattern is idiosyncratic to one firm or is, as the data ultimately suggest, a sector-wide effect.

The econometric framework has two layers. The first is parametric: a Heteroskedasticity and Autocorrelation Consistent (HAC) ordinary least squares regression of the opening gap on overnight FX return and volatility. HAC with Newey-West standard errors is used because financial time series typically exhibit both autocorrelation and time-varying volatility, either of which would invalidate standard OLS inference. The lag window for the Newey-West correction is selected using the Stock-Watson rule of thumb, `m = floor(4 × (n/100)^(2/9))`, which chooses more lags in larger samples and fewer in smaller ones. Two specifications are estimated: a Core specification with FX return and volatility as the only predictors, and a Full specification that adds 2-day and 5-day cumulative returns. Variance Inflation Factors are monitored in both to detect problematic multicollinearity; no VIF exceeded the conventional threshold of 10.

The second layer is non-parametric: a Random Forest regressor for gap magnitude and a Random Forest classifier for gap direction, both with 500 trees and a fixed random seed. These models are evaluated on an out-of-sample test set comprising the last 20% of observations, with a two-business-day embargo between the training tail and the test head to eliminate short-term autocorrelation leakage. Random Forests were chosen over neural networks because the sample size (roughly 153 observations per ticker) is too small to train deep models without severe overfitting, and because tree-based models produce directly interpretable feature importances through the Mean Decrease Impurity metric.

Beyond the point estimates, four diagnostic analyses characterize the temporal structure of the relationship. Cross-correlation functions at lags zero through five test whether the FX-gap relationship is strictly contemporaneous or extends across multiple days. Granger causality F-tests at lags one through five formally test whether past FX returns provide incremental predictive power beyond the gap's own autoregressive structure. A VAR(2) model with orthogonalized impulse response functions traces how a one-standard-deviation FX shock propagates into the gap over a five-day horizon. Rolling expanding-window OOS R² plots evaluate whether the predictive relationship is stable across the sample or concentrated in particular market regimes.

Out-of-sample performance is evaluated against a naive zero-forecast benchmark using the Diebold-Mariano test. Directional accuracy is tested against a 50% null using a one-sided binomial test, and p-values across the seven configurations are corrected for multiple testing via the Holm-Bonferroni procedure.

## What we found

The Core HAC OLS specification produces a standardized FX return coefficient between 0.164 and 0.227 across all seven configurations, with every Newey-West corrected p-value below 0.025. FX volatility is statistically insignificant in all linear specifications, with p-values ranging from 0.58 to 0.94. The Full specification, which adds 2-day and 5-day cumulative returns, does not materially change the picture: the coefficient on 1-day FX return remains significant, and the longer-horizon terms add no meaningful explanatory power.

The cross-correlation functions reveal a distinct peak at lag zero — representing the predictive relationship from the overnight FX window to the next morning's opening gap — with magnitudes between 0.14 and 0.21. At lag one, the correlations flip sign and decay toward zero, a pattern consistent with rapid absorption of information once the Tokyo market opens. The Granger causality tests reinforce this: p-values at lags one through five range from 0.20 to 0.52, and the null of no multi-day causality cannot be rejected at any conventional significance level. The impulse response functions show a significant positive initial response, a directional reversal at step one, and decay toward zero by step four — the signature of a structural information friction rather than a persistent predictive relationship.

Out-of-sample magnitude prediction is poor. OOS R² values are negative across all seven experiments, ranging from -0.065 to -0.178. A naive zero forecast outperforms the Random Forest regressor's point predictions in every case. Mutual information between FX features and gaps peaks at roughly 0.10, a low value that suggests the signal-to-noise ratio on any given day is thin. The Diebold-Mariano statistics are mostly negative (model worse than naive), and only two of seven reach Holm-adjusted significance after correction.

Directional accuracy shows more variation. The Mitsubishi Materials (5711) single-stock model achieves 60.1% directional accuracy on the test set, substantially above the 50% chance baseline and statistically significant by the binomial test. The other two single-stock results, however, are near 50% (49.7% for 5713, 49.0% for 5706), and the multi-stock baskets range from 49.7% to 53.3%. The Mitsubishi result is best interpreted as one outlier among seven configurations, most likely reflecting overfitting or favorable sample variance rather than a robust signal. The paper is explicit on this point in Section 5.4.

The Random Forest feature importance analysis produces an interesting reconciliation with the linear results. Although FX volatility is statistically insignificant in every linear specification, it ranks as the second-most-important feature in the tree models (MDI ≈ 0.20), just behind the autoregressive gap lags. This suggests volatility plays a conditioning role — perhaps moderating how directional FX returns transmit into gap magnitudes — that is invisible to linear regression but detectable to a non-linear model that can capture interaction effects.

## What this does and doesn't mean

The evidence supports a narrow conclusion: overnight USD/JPY returns carry information about the next-morning opening gap of USD-exposed Japanese mining equities, the effect is statistically robust across multiple specifications and firms, and it is tightly bounded to the opening auction itself. The combination of a significant contemporaneous effect, a Granger null at all longer lags, and uniformly negative OOS R² values is consistent with a one-shot information transfer: FX information accumulated while the Tokyo market is closed is priced in at the opening batch auction, after which no residual predictive power remains. This is the "Trading Place Hypothesis" described in Section 2 of the paper, and the paper's empirical pattern is exactly what that hypothesis predicts.

What the evidence does not support is any claim of tradability. The effect's bounded nature — it exists at the open and dissipates immediately — means that capturing it would require intraday execution capability that daily-frequency data cannot inform. The negative OOS R² values, consistent signs of overfitting, and weak directional accuracy outside the Mitsubishi outlier mean that naive strategies that simply "buy on positive FX overnight, sell at close" would not be expected to produce consistent returns after transaction costs. Section 7 of the paper sketches a more sophisticated approach via quanto derivative pricing, which would isolate the equity-specific repricing signal from spot FX exposure; but that framework is presented as future work, not as a validated result.

## Why we are showing this anyway

An obvious question is why publish a paper whose headline out-of-sample result is that the model cannot beat a zero forecast. The answer is that the contribution is methodological, not economic. The paper demonstrates that a carefully-identified lead-lag hypothesis — operationalized with a strict timestamp-level lookahead guard, tested across seven configurations, reconciled across linear and non-linear specifications, and evaluated with the full suite of modern time-series diagnostics — can rigorously characterize an information friction without overclaiming. The boundedness of the effect is the finding. The negative OOS R² is not a bug to be hidden; it is evidence that the effect is doing exactly what a properly-identified information friction should do, decaying immediately upon the opening of the equity market.

For a reader evaluating this work on the terms of a research contribution, the methodological discipline matters at least as much as the substantive finding. A paper that reported 60% directional accuracy across all seven configurations and claimed tradability would be suspicious — the effect being studied is too local to the opening auction for that to be plausible. The paper's internal consistency — significant contemporaneous effect, null Granger causality, negative OOS R², sign reversal in the impulse response — is what gives the substantive conclusion its weight.

## Where this would go next

Three extensions would materially strengthen the analysis. First, intraday equity data would allow direct estimation of how quickly the contemporaneous effect absorbs into prices once the Tokyo market reopens — currently inferrable from the impulse response function's step-one reversal but not directly measurable at the daily frequency. Second, the quanto pricing framework sketched in Section 7 would provide a concrete mechanism for isolating the repricing signal from spot FX exposure; this is the natural next step if the effect were to be translated into any strategy. Third, the analysis should be replicated across other sectors with similar USD-revenue / local-currency-cost asymmetries — Japanese automobile manufacturers, Korean semiconductor firms, Brazilian iron ore miners — to test whether the information friction is a feature of Japanese mining specifically or a broader phenomenon at the boundary between 24-hour FX markets and local equity auctions.
